Building a Support Network

About this module

Eczema affects more than your skin. It can shape your mood, your sleep, your social life, and how you feel about yourself. You do not have to manage all of that alone. This module is about building a team around you, knowing when to ask for extra help, and finding the resources you can actually trust.

Key points

Read the key points Hide the key points
  • Eczema can affect your emotional well-being, your relationships, your work, and your self-esteem.
  • It is normal to feel embarrassed, frustrated, or down sometimes about your skin.
  • It may be time to ask for extra help if you feel down most days, are avoiding people, are sleeping or eating poorly, or feel hopeless.
  • Your family doctor or dermatologist can refer you to mental health support.
  • Your support team can include doctors, counsellors, family, friends, online communities, and peers who share your experience.
  • You can ask the people in your life for specific kinds of help — like reminders for treatments or help avoiding triggers.
  • At work or school, you can choose what to share. Disclosure is your call.
  • If someone asks an unkind question: "It's just eczema. It's a common skin condition. It's not contagious."
  • Reliable Canadian resources: Eczema Society of Canada (eczemahelp.ca) and the National Eczema Association (nationaleczema.org).

AHS Mental Health Helpline — available 24/7 in Alberta

Call or text: 1-877-303-2642

Bring this to your appointment

  • "Lately I've been feeling low about my eczema — can we talk about it?"
  • "Can you refer me to a counsellor or mental health support?"
  • "I'd like to talk about how eczema is affecting my relationships."
  • "What community resources do you recommend for people with eczema?"
