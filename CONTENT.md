## module-1a.html — Understanding Your Skin-Mind Connection
Accent: #C2607A

Intro:
Eczema is more than a skin condition. Stress and emotions can make symptoms
worse, and eczema can affect how you feel day to day — your sleep, your mood,
and how you show up at work or with people you care about. This module is about
that connection between your mind and your skin, what we now understand about it,
and what your dermatologist can do to help.

YouTube video ID: VIDEO_ID

Key points:
- Eczema is a "psychophysiologic" condition. Stress doesn't cause eczema, but
  it can make symptoms worse.
- When your body is stressed, it releases hormones like cortisol that affect
  both your immune system and your skin barrier.
- This can lead to more inflammation, more itching, and more flares.
- Eczema can affect your sleep, your daily activities, your work or studies,
  and how you feel emotionally.
- Adults with eczema are more likely to experience anxiety or low mood than
  people without eczema.
- This is not a sign of weakness. It is a normal response to a chronic condition.
- Your dermatologist can help with both the physical and emotional sides of eczema.
- Effective support is available, and asking for it is part of good care.

Bring this to your appointment:
- "I'd like to talk about how stress is affecting my skin."
- "I've noticed my eczema gets worse during certain times — can we work on
  that together?"
- "I'm interested in support for my mood as well as my skin care."
- "What do you recommend for stress-related flares?"

## module-2.html — Breaking the Itch-Scratch Cycle
Accent: #2E7D5E

Intro:
For many people with eczema, the itch is the hardest part. It disrupts sleep,
ruins concentration, and feeds a cycle that keeps the skin damaged. This module
covers why the itch happens, why it gets worse at night, and practical ways to
break the scratching habit — including techniques you can start using today.

YouTube video ID: VIDEO_ID

Key points:
- Itch leads to scratching. Scratching damages the skin. Damaged skin itches
  more. This is the itch-scratch cycle.
- Emotional triggers like stress, tiredness, and worry can make itch feel worse.
- Physical triggers include heat, sweat, rough fabrics, fragrances, and dust.
- Itch often gets worse at night because there are fewer distractions and your
  skin becomes more sensitive.
- Habit Reversal Therapy (HRT) teaches your hands a different response to the
  urge to scratch — like making a fist or applying firm pressure.
- The "Soak and Seal" method: warm (not hot) bath for 5–10 minutes, pat skin
  damp, apply thick moisturizer within 3 minutes.
- Lotions are often too watery for eczema. Thick creams or ointments work better.
- Wet wrap therapy can help during severe flares. Ask your doctor before trying
  it at home.
- Keeping nails short and wearing soft cotton gloves at night can reduce damage
  from sleep scratching.

Bring this to your appointment:
- "Night-time itching is affecting my sleep — what can we do?"
- "I'd like to try Habit Reversal Therapy. Can you walk me through it or
  refer me?"
- "Would wet wrap therapy be appropriate for my flares?"
- "I think a specific trigger is making my itch worse — can we talk about it?"

## module-3a.html — Building Your Self-Management Skills & Confidence
Accent: #6B4FA0

Intro:
Living well with eczema means taking an active role in your own care. That
doesn't mean doing it alone — it means knowing your triggers, setting goals
that fit your life, recovering from setbacks without giving up, and speaking
up for what you need. This module is about building those skills and the
confidence that comes with them.

YouTube video ID: VIDEO_ID

Key points:
- Self-efficacy means believing you can manage your eczema. Believing it makes
  you more likely to do it.
- Tracking your symptoms, foods, products, activities, and stress helps you see
  patterns over time.
- Apps like EczemaWise (from the National Eczema Association) make tracking
  easier on your phone.
- Small consistent habits beat big perfect plans. Start with one thing for
  one week.
- "Habit stacking" links a new habit to something you already do — like
  moisturizing right after brushing your teeth.
- SMART goals are Specific, Measurable, Achievable, Relevant, and Time-bound.
- Setbacks are part of the journey, not a sign of failure. Treat them as
  information.
- The way you talk to yourself matters. "This flare will pass" works better
  than "this will never get better."
- An Eczema Action Plan, written with your doctor, lays out your daily care,
  your early flare signs, your step-up plan, and when to call.

Bring this to your appointment:
- "I'd like to create an Eczema Action Plan with you."
- "I've been tracking my symptoms — here's what I've noticed."
- "I have one specific question about my treatment today."
- "Can we set a goal together for the next few weeks?"

## module-4.html — Building a Support Network
Accent: #3A7D8C

Intro:
Eczema affects more than your skin. It can shape your mood, your sleep, your
social life, and how you feel about yourself. You do not have to manage all of
that alone. This module is about building a team around you, knowing when to
ask for extra help, and finding the resources you can actually trust.

YouTube video ID: VIDEO_ID

Key points:
- Eczema can affect your emotional well-being, your relationships, your work,
  and your self-esteem.
- It is normal to feel embarrassed, frustrated, or down sometimes about
  your skin.
- It may be time to ask for extra help if you feel down most days, are avoiding
  people, are sleeping or eating poorly, or feel hopeless.
- Your family doctor or dermatologist can refer you to mental health support.
- Your support team can include doctors, counsellors, family, friends, online
  communities, and peers who share your experience.
- You can ask the people in your life for specific kinds of help — like
  reminders for treatments or help avoiding triggers.
- At work or school, you can choose what to share. Disclosure is your call.
- If someone asks an unkind question: "It's just eczema. It's a common skin
  condition. It's not contagious."
- Reliable Canadian resources: Eczema Society of Canada (eczemahelp.ca) and
  the National Eczema Association (nationaleczema.org).

CALLOUT BOX (visually distinct — not a bullet point):
AHS Mental Health Helpline — available 24/7 in Alberta
Call or text: 1-877-303-2642

Bring this to your appointment:
- "Lately I've been feeling low about my eczema — can we talk about it?"
- "Can you refer me to a counsellor or mental health support?"
- "I'd like to talk about how eczema is affecting my relationships."
- "What community resources do you recommend for people with eczema?"

## module-5.html — Holistic Skin Care and Wellness
Accent: #C27C2E

Intro:
Managing eczema well means more than treating flares. What you eat, how you
move, how you sleep, and how you handle stress all affect your skin. This
module brings those pieces together — with practical strategies you can start
using this week, even on your busiest days.

YouTube video ID: VIDEO_ID

Key points:
- A "good enough" routine beats a perfect routine you can't keep up. Simplify
  during stressful weeks rather than stopping entirely.
- Flare prevention starts with knowing your early warning signs and having a
  plan ready before a flare fully develops.
- Mindfulness means paying attention to the present moment without judgment.
  Even five minutes a day can reduce stress and scratch urges.
- Progressive Muscle Relaxation (PMR) — tensing and releasing muscle groups
  one at a time — can lower stress and help you sleep.
- Cognitive Behavioural Therapy (CBT) has been shown to reduce itch intensity,
  sleep problems, anxiety, and depression in people with eczema.
- A skin-friendly diet is an anti-inflammatory diet: more fruits, vegetables,
  and whole grains; less processed food and added sugar.
- No single food causes eczema. Talk to your doctor before cutting out any
  food group.
- Regular moderate exercise lowers cortisol, improves sleep, and supports mood.
- During flares, choose lower-sweat activities and shower with lukewarm water
  afterward.
- Alberta-based resources: MindShift CBT app, BounceBack Alberta, AHS Mental
  Health Helpline (1-877-303-2642), and your family doctor for referrals.

Bring this to your appointment:
- "I'd like help building a 'good enough' routine for stressful weeks."
- "Could CBT help with my itch and sleep, and how would I access it here?"
- "I want to add more movement to my week — what works with active eczema?"
- "Can we talk about how stress and my skin are connected for me specifically?"

## clinician.html — For Clinicians

Body:
MindSkin Connect is a digital home for the Mind-Skin Health for Atopic
Dermatitis (MSHAD) patient education modules, developed by the University of
Alberta Division of Dermatology. The platform is designed for adults and older
teens managing their own eczema, and is shared with patients via QR code at
participating clinics.

The platform delivers five modules:
- Module 1a — Understanding Your Skin-Mind Connection
- Module 2 — Breaking the Itch-Scratch Cycle
- Module 3a — Building Your Self-Management Skills & Confidence
- Module 4 — Building a Support Network
- Module 5 — Holistic Skin Care and Wellness

Each module ends with patient-voice prompts ("Bring this to your appointment")
that patients may choose to raise during follow-up visits. These are designed
to support shared decision-making, not to direct it.

For questions about the platform or the underlying MSHAD program, please
contact the program team at supportg@ualberta.ca.

Tie (two or more share top score):

  3a before 4, 4 before 5). First = primary, second = secondary.

All equal or all the same low score:
→ show neutral result screen (see below). Do not show a primary
  recommendation.

Q6 modifier rules:
