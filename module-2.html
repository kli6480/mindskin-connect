Breaking the Itch-Scratch Cycle

About this module

For many people with eczema, the itch is the hardest part. It disrupts sleep, ruins concentration, and feeds a cycle that keeps the skin damaged. This module covers why the itch happens, why it gets worse at night, and practical ways to break the scratching habit — including techniques you can start using today.

Key points

Read the key points Hide the key points
  • Itch leads to scratching. Scratching damages the skin. Damaged skin itches more. This is the itch-scratch cycle.
  • Emotional triggers like stress, tiredness, and worry can make itch feel worse.
  • Physical triggers include heat, sweat, rough fabrics, fragrances, and dust.
  • Itch often gets worse at night because there are fewer distractions and your skin becomes more sensitive.
  • Habit Reversal Therapy (HRT) teaches your hands a different response to the urge to scratch — like making a fist or applying firm pressure.
  • The "Soak and Seal" method: warm (not hot) bath for 5–10 minutes, pat skin damp, apply thick moisturizer within 3 minutes.
  • Lotions are often too watery for eczema. Thick creams or ointments work better.
  • Wet wrap therapy can help during severe flares. Ask your doctor before trying it at home.
  • Keeping nails short and wearing soft cotton gloves at night can reduce damage from sleep scratching.

Bring this to your appointment

  • "Night-time itching is affecting my sleep — what can we do?"
  • "I'd like to try Habit Reversal Therapy. Can you walk me through it or refer me?"
  • "Would wet wrap therapy be appropriate for my flares?"
  • "I think a specific trigger is making my itch worse — can we talk about it?"
